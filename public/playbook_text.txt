Hiring Playbook
Section 1 — Purpose & Philosophy
DIME Interview & Hiring Handbook
1. Why This Exists
Hiring at DIME is not about filling roles. It is about deciding who gets to build this company with us. Every person we bring 
in will either make execution easier or harder, clearer or more confusing. Bad hiring doesn’t fail immediately; it shows up 
over time in missed ownership, slow decisions, and growing friction inside the team. Good hiring does the opposite. It 
raises the level of everyone around it. This handbook exists to keep our decisions consistent and intentional, especially 
when things get busy.
2. What We Care About
When you speak to a candidate, don’t try to evaluate everything at once. Focus on a simple order. First, do they actually 
care about what we’re building, or are they just looking for a job? Then, do they take responsibility, or do they wait for 
direction? After that, look at how they think. Can they break down a problem clearly, or do they just speak in general terms? 
Then notice their behavior — would working with them feel smooth or difficult? Only after that comes skills. Skills matter, 
but they don’t come first.
3. What We Don’t Compromise On
There are things we don’t ignore, no matter how strong the candidate looks on paper. If someone keeps blaming others, 
avoids responsibility, speaks in a disrespectful way, or gives generic answers without depth, we stop. The same applies if 
they show no real interest in DIME itself. These signals don’t improve later. If anything, they become more visible with time.
4. What “Good” Looks Like
A strong candidate is usually easy to understand. They explain things clearly, give real examples instead of opinions, and 
are comfortable talking about mistakes without trying to hide them. You can see how they think, not just what they say. 
They ask questions that show curiosity, not just basic information. Most importantly, they feel real. You don’t need to guess 
what they mean.
5. What “Weak” Looks Like
A weak candidate often sounds good at first but becomes unclear when you go deeper. They speak in general statements, 
avoid specifics, or give answers that sound perfect but lack real situations behind them. They may talk a lot but say very 
little. When asked about challenges or mistakes, they either shift responsibility or keep the story surface-level. Over time, 
you start feeling that something is missing, even if you can’t name it immediately.
6. Your Role as an Interviewer
Your role is not to impress the candidate or to sell DIME. It is also not to fill the role as fast as possible. Your role is to 
understand who is sitting in front of you. Try to answer three simple questions: would you trust this person with something 
important, do they actually think or just speak well, and will they make the team stronger or heavier? If you stay focused on 
these questions, your decisions will be much clearer.
7. How to Decide
At the end of the interview, keep the decision simple. If the person showed real ownership, gave clear and specific 
answers, and you did not see major red flags, then it makes sense to move forward. If things felt vague, forced, or unclear, 
or if you noticed weak ownership, then it’s better to stop early. Ignoring small doubts usually leads to bigger problems later.

Section 2 — Hiring Principles (Non-Negotiables)
DIME Interview & Hiring Handbook
1. How We Approach Hiring
At DIME, hiring is not about finding the “best candidate on paper.” It is about finding the right person for how we work.
We don’t assume that a strong CV means strong performance. We don’t assume confidence means competence. And we 
don’t assume experience means ownership.
Instead, we look for patterns in behavior. What someone has done repeatedly tells us more than what they say they can do.
Every interview should feel like an attempt to understand the person, not to confirm a good impression.
2. We Evaluate Behavior, Not Opinions
Candidates will often give opinions about how they work, lead, or solve problems. That is not enough.
What matters is what they have actually done.
When a candidate says, “I’m good at handling pressure” or “I take ownership,” your job is to move the conversation to real 
situations. Ask for examples. Ask what happened, what they did, and what the result was.
If they cannot move from general اﻟﻜلامto a real story, that is a signal.
At DIME, we trust actions more than statements.
3. We Go One Layer Deeper
Most interviews stay on the surface. We don’t.
When a candidate gives an answer, don’t stop at the first layer. Ask simple follow-ups:
What exactly was your role?
What did you do when things went wrong?
What would you do differently now?
You are not trying to challenge the candidate. You are trying to see how real the answer is.
Clear thinking becomes more visible the deeper you go. Weak answers usually break under simple follow-up questions.
4. We Look for Patterns, Not One Good Answer
One strong answer does not mean a strong candidate.
People can prepare stories. They can rehearse answers. But it is very hard to fake consistency.
Across the interview, pay attention to patterns:
Do they consistently take ownership, or only in one story?

But hiring fast and hiring right are not the same.
A wrong hire creates more delay than an empty seat.
If a candidate is “almost good” but not clearly right, we wait. It is better to take longer than to fix a bad decision later.
8. We Protect the Team, Not Just the Role
When you evaluate someone, don’t only think about the role they will fill.
Think about the team they will join.
Will this person make collaboration easier or harder?
Will they raise the level of thinking in the team?
Will others trust working with them?
A technically strong person who weakens the team is not a good hire.
9. We Stay Honest in Both Directions
Interviews are not one-sided.
Just as we expect honesty from candidates, we should be honest about DIME.

Section 3 — Hiring Scorecard System
DIME Interview & Hiring Handbook
1. Why We Use a Scorecard
Interviews can easily turn into opinions. One person likes the candidate, another doesn’t, and the decision becomes 
unclear.
The scorecard exists to fix that.
It gives every interviewer the same structure to think through the candidate. It keeps the conversation focused on 
evidence, not impressions. It also makes sure we are evaluating what actually matters to DIME, not personal preferences.
The goal is not to make hiring mechanical. The goal is to make it clear and consistent.
2. What We Evaluate
Every candidate is evaluated across five areas. These areas reflect how we work at DIME and what we need from people.
We look first at belief. Does this person actually care about what we are building, or are they just looking for the next 
opportunity? This is what drives commitment over time.
Then we look at ownership. Do they take responsibility and move things forward, or do they wait for direction? This is what 
drives execution.
Then we look at how they think. Can they break down problems, explain clearly, and deal with ambiguity? This is what 
drives good decisions.
Then we look at behavior. How do they work with others? Do they listen, communicate clearly, and make the team easier to 
work with?
Finally, we look at skills. Can they do the job now, or can they grow into it quickly?
This order matters. Skills alone are not enough.
3. How We Score
Each area is scored from 1 to 5.
A score of 1 means there is a clear problem or misalignment. A score of 3 means the candidate is acceptable but not 
strong. A score of 5 means the signal is clear and strong.
The score should always be based on what you heard and observed, not what you assume.
If you cannot justify the score with a real example from the interview, the score is not valid.
4. What Strong Scores Look Like

A high score in belief means the candidate speaks about the problem in a real and grounded way. You can see why it

A high score in ownership means they give clear examples of taking responsibility, especially when things were not going

A high score in thinking means they can explain things simply, break problems into steps, and stay clear under follow-up

A high score in behavior means they listen, respond thoughtfully, and show respect in how they speak about others.
A high score in skills means they have either done similar work before or can clearly show how they would approach it.
5. What Weak Scores Look Like
Low scores usually show up as vagueness.
In belief, it sounds like generic motivation without depth.
In ownership, it shows as shared responsibility with no clear role.
In thinking, it becomes unclear or messy when you go deeper.
In behavior, it may show as ego, defensiveness, or lack of awareness.
In skills, it appears as theory without practical understanding.
Weak signals are often not loud. They appear as a lack of clarity.
6. Red Flags Override the Score
Even if a candidate scores well in some areas, certain signals override everything.
If you see consistent blaming, lack of responsibility, disrespectful behavior, or clear lack of interest in DIME, you do not 
move forward.
The scorecard supports the decision, but it does not replace judgment.
7. How to Use the Scorecard After the Interview
Right after the interview, each interviewer should fill the scorecard individually.
Do not discuss with others before writing your evaluation. This keeps the input independent.
For each area, give a score and write one short reason based on what you observed.
Then make a clear decision: move forward or not.
Avoid long justifications. If the signal is not clear, that is already part of the answer.
8. How Decisions Are Made
When interviewers come together, the goal is not to debate opinions. The goal is to compare observations.
Each person shares:
their decision
the strongest signal they saw
the main concern they have
If there is disagreement, go back to evidence. What exactly did the candidate say or do?
If the decision is still unclear, we do not move forward.
9. Final Rule
The scorecard is there to guide thinking, not to force a decision.

Section 4 — Interview Process & Flow
DIME Interview & Hiring Handbook
1. Why the Process Matters
Hiring should not depend on who is interviewing or how the day is going.
The process exists to make sure every candidate goes through the same logic. It keeps things fair, reduces rushed 
decisions, and helps us focus on the right signals at the right time.
Each stage has a clear purpose. We don’t try to evaluate everything in one interview.
2. Overview of the Hiring Flow
At DIME, the hiring process is simple and structured.
A candidate moves through four main steps:
First interview (25 minutes)
Deep interview (around 1 hour)
Role-based case or simulation
Final discussion and decision
Not every candidate reaches all stages. Each step is a filter.
We only invest more time when the signal is strong enough.
3. Purpose of Each Stage
The first interview is a quick filter. It is used to understand the person at a high level. We focus on belief, ownership, clarity, 
and basic role fit. The goal is not to go deep. The goal is to decide if this person is worth a deeper conversation.
The deep interview is where we understand behavior in detail. We explore real past situations, not opinions. We look for 
patterns in how the candidate works, learns, and handles challenges.
The case or simulation tests how the candidate thinks in practice. It shows how they approach real problems, not just how 
they describe them.
The final discussion is where we step back and decide. We bring all inputs together and make a clear decision based on 
evidence.
4. Who Is Responsible in Each Stage
Each stage should have a clear owner.
The first interview is usually led by People & Culture or the hiring manager. The goal here is consistency.
The deep interview is led by the hiring manager or functional lead. This is where deeper judgment is required.
The case or simulation is handled by someone from the function, or a small group, depending on the role.
The final decision involves the hiring manager and relevant stakeholders, but it should stay focused and not become a long 
debate.
At every stage, each interviewer is responsible for writing their own evaluation before discussing with others.

5. When a Candidate Moves Forward
A candidate moves to the next stage only if the signal is clear enough.
We are not looking for perfect answers. We are looking for strong indicators.
If the candidate shows real ownership, clear thinking, and no major red flags, we move forward.
If things feel average, unclear, or forced, we stop early.
This keeps the pipeline clean and saves time for both sides.
6. When We Stop the Process
Stopping early is not a failure. It is part of the system.
We stop when:
answers are vague or inconsistent
ownership is weak
belief is not clear
there are red flags in behavior
It is better to stop after 25 minutes than to continue for hours with a weak signal.
7. Keeping the Process Consistent
Every interviewer should follow the same structure, even if their style is different.
The goal is not to ask the exact same questions every time, but to reach the same understanding:
who the person is
how they think
how they act
Consistency in process leads to better decisions over time.
8. Communication with Candidates
We keep communication simple and respectful.
If a candidate moves forward, we inform them clearly and quickly.
If not, we close the process without delay. No long waiting or unclear status.
The way we communicate reflects how we operate.
9. Final Thought
The process is not there to slow us down.
It is there to protect us from rushed decisions.
A clear process makes hiring faster in the long run, because we spend time only on the right candidates.

You are observing thinking, not listening for keywords.
9. Common Interviewer Mistakes
Do not:
guide them too much
correct them during the case
turn it into teaching
look for your own answer
Let them show how they think, even if it’s not perfect.
10. Decision After the Case
After this stage, your view should be clear.
Ask yourself:
can this person handle real problems at DIME?
do they think in a structured way?
do they stay clear under pressure?
If the answer is not clearly yes, do not move forward.
11. Final Standard
This stage is where standards go up.
A candidate who passed earlier stages but fails here
should not move forward.
Because at DIME:
execution matters more than impression.

DIME Interview & Hiring Handbook
1. Your Role in the Interview
Your role is not to run a conversation.
Your role is to understand the person.
You are trying to see:
how they think
how they act
how real their answers are
You are not there to impress them, help them, or fill the role quickly.
If you stay focused on understanding, your questions will naturally improve.
2. Ask for Real Examples, Not Opinions
Candidates will often speak in general terms.
When that happens, bring them back to reality.
If they say, “I handle pressure well,” ask:
“Tell me about a time that actually happened.”
Always move from:
what they say
to
what they did
If they cannot give a clear example, treat that as a signal.
3. Keep Questions Simple
Don’t try to sound smart.

4. Go One Step Deeper
The first answer is rarely enough.

“Why did you choose that?”

5. Let Silence Work
Don’t rush to fill silence.
If the candidate pauses, let them think.
Strong candidates use that time to structure their answer.
Weak candidates become more unclear.
Silence helps you see the difference.
6. Don’t Lead the Candidate
Avoid helping them reach a “better” answer.
Don’t suggest ideas.
Don’t complete their thoughts.
If you guide too much, you are no longer evaluating them — you are co-creating the answer.
Let them show their own thinking.
7. Separate Confidence from Clarity
Some candidates speak confidently but lack depth.
Don’t confuse delivery with quality.
To test this, slow them down:
ask them to explain step by step
ask them to simplify
If they truly understand, they will become clearer.
If not, the answer will break.

Pay attention to how they speak, not just what they say.
